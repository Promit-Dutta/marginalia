A Functional Equation

May 27, 2026 · 7 min read · Promit Dutta

This problem is from the ISI BMath and BStat 2026 UGB question paper, like the polynomial problem I posted previously.

Problem

Problem. Suppose $f:\mathbb{R}\to\mathbb{R}$ is a twice differentiable function satisfying $$f(x+y)+f(x-y) = 2\bigl(f(x)+f(y)\bigr) \quad \forall\, x,y \in \mathbb{R}.$$

(a) If $f'$ denotes the derivative of $f$, show that $$f'(x+y)-f'(x-y) = 2f'(y) \quad \forall\, x,y \in \mathbb{R}.$$

(b) Further assume $f''(0)=2$, where $f''$ is the second derivative of $f$. Determine $f$.

Solution

Part (a)

The key observation is that if $x$ is held fixed, differentiating both sides with respect to $y$ yields the desired identity directly. This motivates choosing an arbitrary $x^* \in \mathbb{R}$; the functional equation then reads $$f(x^*+y)+f(x^*-y) = 2\bigl(f(x^*)+f(y)\bigr) \quad \forall\, y \in \mathbb{R}.$$

Differentiating both sides with respect to $y$: $$f'(x^*+y)-f'(x^*-y) = 2f'(y) \quad \forall\, y \in \mathbb{R}.$$ Since $x^*$ was chosen arbitrarily, this holds for every $x\in\mathbb{R}$, giving $$f'(x+y)-f'(x-y) = 2f'(y) \quad \forall\, x,y \in \mathbb{R}. \qquad \square$$

Part (b)

Setting $y=0$ in the original equation gives $2f(x)=2f(x)+2f(0)$, so $f(0)=0$. Setting $y=0$ in the identity from part (a) gives $0=2f'(0)$, so $f'(0)=0$.

Differentiating the identity from part (a) with respect to $x$: $$f''(x+y)-f''(x-y) = 0 \quad \forall\, x,y \in \mathbb{R}.$$ Setting $x=y$ gives $f''(2x)=f''(0)=2$ for every $x\in\mathbb{R}$. As $x$ ranges over $\mathbb{R}$, so does $2x$, so $f''$ is identically $2$. Integrating twice: $$f'(x) = 2x+c_1, \qquad f(x) = x^2+c_1 x+c_2.$$ The conditions $f'(0)=0$ and $f(0)=0$ force $c_1=c_2=0$.

Conclusion. The unique function satisfying all the given conditions is $f(x)=x^2$.
