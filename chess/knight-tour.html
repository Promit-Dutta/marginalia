The mathematics behind it

Card 1
What's a graph, really?
A graph is just a collection of points and lines joining some of those points. On a chessboard, each square becomes a point, and we draw a line whenever a knight can move from one square to another in a single move. That simple idea is enough to turn the knight's tour into a graph problem.
Card 2
The tour is a path through the graph
A knight’s tour is a route that visits every square exactly once. If the last square also connects back to the first, the tour is closed. In graph language, that is called a Hamiltonian cycle.
Card 3
Why some boards cannot work
A knight always switches square color with every move. So on a closed tour, the two colors have to balance out perfectly. That already rules out every odd-by-odd board, and Schwenk's theorem goes further by giving the full list of rectangular boards that do or do not admit a closed tour.
Card 4
How we actually search for one
A very common way to look for a tour is Warnsdorff's rule: from the current square, move to the square with the fewest onward moves. It sounds almost too simple, but it works surprisingly well in practice, especially when paired with backtracking if a dead end appears.
